Optical Proximity Correction (OPC)

Description

Lithography is the process of printing tiny circuit patterns onto silicon wafers. Light travels through a mask at different angles, controlled by the illumination source, onto a light-sensitive surface that records the pattern. The problem is that at the scales modern chips operate at, light bends and interferes with itself, causing patterns to blur, round off and distort by the time they reach the wafer (see this video). What you design is never quite what gets printed. Take a simple example, using a desired pattern directly as the mask: Using Fourier optics, we can describe this behaviour precisely. Given a mask and illumination source, the model can predict the resulting intensity pattern and final resist profile on the wafer. The challenge is going the other way: given a desired resist profile, find the mask and illumination that produce it. For a model this complex, that inversion is far from straightforward, and this project tackles it using a neural network and gradient-based optimisation.

Simulating

Using the Fourier optics model, we simulate the full imaging process step by step. The mask is transformed into frequency space, where each frequency represents a spatial detail of the pattern. The illumination source controls which angles of light hit the mask, and for each of those angles a pupil filter removes the finest details that the physical lens doesn't have the resolution to reproduce, similar to how a camera lens with a low aperture produces a blurry image. All these contributions are summed and transformed back to give the intensity pattern that lands on the wafer. Finally, the photoresist, which is the light-sensitive material on the wafer, responds to this intensity with a threshold effect: regions that receive enough light get exposed and regions that don't stay unexposed, giving the final binary result.

The simulator is accurate but slow, and more importantly not differentiable, meaning we can't use it directly to optimise the mask and illumination through gradient descent. That's where the surrogate model comes in.

Surrogate Neural Network

We can train a neural network to approximate the simulator by generating mask and illumination pairs and using the Fourier optics model to compute the ground truth wafer intensity and resist profile for each one. The architecture is a UNet, a natural choice for image-to-image prediction tasks. It processes the mask through a series of convolutional layers that capture local spatial patterns, compressing it down to a compact representation and then expanding back up to full resolution. Convolutional layers are well suited here since the mask is inherently spatial, and what matters at each pixel depends strongly on its local neighbourhood. Skip connections pass feature maps directly from the encoder to the corresponding decoder layer, giving the network a shortcut to fine spatial detail that would otherwise be lost during compression. U The illumination is handled differently. Unlike the mask, the illumination source is a global property of the optical system, affecting how every part of the mask gets resolved on the wafer equally. Feeding it in as just another input channel and processing it with convolutions doesn't reflect that. Instead we use Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM): the illumination is encoded into a compact vector, which is used to dynamically scale and shift the feature maps at each level of the network. It is similar to a standard layer in a neural network, but rather than having fixed learned parameters, the scale and shift values are predicted on the fly from the illumination input, adapting the network's behaviour to each illumination pattern. Once trained, gradients can flow through the surrogate, meaning we can use it to work backwards from a desired resist profile to find the mask and illumination that produce it.

Data Generation

To train the surrogate, we need a large and diverse dataset of mask, illumination and simulated output pairs. We start with a set of 4000 base circuit patterns, but that alone wouldn't give the network enough variety to generalise well. During optimisation, the network will encounter mask and illumination combinations it has never seen before. If those combinations fall outside the training distribution, the network's predictions become unreliable, and the optimiser ends up exploiting these blind spots rather than finding physically meaningful solutions. To fix this, we apply a range of augmentations to generate thousands of variations including dilation, erosion, boundary noise, corner rounding and random feature additions, producing masks that could resemble OPC-corrected patterns. For the illumination, we generate random source patterns by placing Gaussian modes at different positions in frequency space, and blending them with random weights. Each mask and illumination pair is passed through the simulator to generate ground truth intensity and resist profiles, giving us a dataset of around 50k training samples to start with.

Gradient-Based Optimisation

With a trained surrogate, we can now optimise the mask and illumination jointly. Because the surrogate is differentiable, we can compute gradients of the resist prediction with respect to both the mask and the illumination, and use those gradients to find adjustments that bring the predicted output closer to the desired pattern. The optimisation starts from the target pattern itself as the initial mask, with a randomly sampled illumination. To avoid getting stuck in poor local minima, we apply a Gaussian blur to the mask that anneals over iterations, starting coarse and gradually sharpening. This lets the optimiser find the right global structure before committing to fine details. A binary phase at the end pushes mask values toward 0 or 1, since real masks are binary. [PLACEHOLDER: final result grid showing target, optimised mask, illumination, NN predicted resist] The surrogate prediction matches the target closely, and the optimised mask and illumination look physically meaningful. However, when we verify the result using the ground truth simulator, a different picture emerges. The actual printed result differs significantly from what the surrogate predicted. The optimiser found a solution that looks good to the network, but that solution sits outside the region the network was trained on, where its predictions can no longer be trusted.

Active Learning

The optimiser is very good at finding masks and illuminations that fool the surrogate into predicting a good result. But when we simulate those optimised inputs with the ground truth simulator, the actual printed result is often quite different. The optimiser has pushed the inputs outside the distribution the network was trained on, into regions where the surrogate is confidently wrong. [PLACEHOLDER: example showing NN prediction looks great but litho sim disagrees] The solution is active learning. Instead of treating the training dataset as fixed, we use the optimisation process itself to generate new training data. Snapshots of the mask taken throughout each optimisation run are collected and simulated with the ground truth simulator to get accurate labels. These new samples are added to the training set and the surrogate is retrained, making it more accurate in exactly the regions the optimiser cares about. This loop repeats, with each round of optimisation generating better training data for the next. Over 20 rounds, adding around 2500 new samples each time, the surrogate learns to accurately predict the simulator output for optimised masks, and the gap between surrogate prediction and ground truth closes significantly.

Results

After active learning, the surrogate predictions and ground truth simulator results are in much closer agreement.
